A GIGANTIC MAP OF THE UNIVERSE

 

Some of the minds wobbling questions in this respect are the following: -

What does the universe look like on the very largest scales?

How much matter does it contain?

What are the biggest things that exist?

To answer such questions, astronomers are measuring the redshifts (and thus distances) of thousands of galaxies to map gigantic slices of the universe in three dimensions.

 

The largest such survey to date is the 2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey, being carried out with the 4-meter Anglo-Australian Telescope in Australia. So far it has measured the redshifts of 106,000 galaxies, four times more than any previous effort. They lie within two pizza-slice wedges of sky about 3-deg. thick, 90-deg. wide and extending out some 2 billion light-years from Earth, to a redshift of 0.25. The survey's goal is to collect 250,000 galaxy redshifts. Team members Gavin B. Dalton (Oxford University) and Karl Glazebrook (Johns Hopkins University) presented a map of the wedges of space surveyed to date.

 

Seen on such a gigantic scale, the cosmos is a filamentary network of galaxy clusters, superclusters, voids, and "great walls" up to 500 million light-years long. Never before has a three-dimensional map spanned so many of them. One result the astronomers announced was that they found the largest structures that exist. At last, they have a map of space large enough to see that there are no structures as big as the map itself.

 

Another result is even more important: a newly reliable measure of the total density of matter in the universe, based on the gravity exerted by the largest galaxy clusters. "The idea is simple," said Dalton. "Near large concentrations of galaxies, the expansion of the universe is slowed down by their gravitational pull. We can measure this slowing and estimate the mass of the concentrations. By analyzing the results of the whole survey, we can directly measure the mass of the universe."

 

Their result? The density of all matter comes to 40 percent, plus or minus 10 percent, of the density that would be needed to render space "flat." The inflation theory of the Big Bang predicts that space is flat, and flatness as dramatically proven by two balloon-borne experiments last month. The mass measured by the 2dF team doesn't measure up -- in agreement with many other groups' previous results. The team determined the gravity of both ordinary matter and the larger amount of "dark matter," made of some kind of unknown particles, that resides in galaxy clusters. This does not, however, include the recently discovered "dark energy" (the so-called cosmological constant or vacuum energy) that apparently exists in just the right amount to make up the deficit and bring the total density to 1.0.

 

The 2dF survey is not the only great cosmic mapping program underway. The Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS) is working in parallel to map different parts of the sky to complete our understanding of the "look and feel" of our universe.
