The 16th and 17th Century...

       Tycho Brahe was a Danish astronomer born in 1546. He was a brilliant observer and amassed huge amounts of data on planetary motions. Unfortunately for him he was a believer in the geocentric theory and never looked at his work in another light. There goes an interesting story about him - he had a rather noticeable nose. Whilst at university he ended up in a duel with a fellow student and lost a part of his nose. He replaced the lost part with a curious mixture of gold, silver and wax ! When he died in 1601, his young assistant Johannes Kepler took over his work.

       Johannes Kepler started to analyze some of the data and realised the only fit to the data came when one adopted the heliocentric theory. As a result he published three laws of planetary motion which revolutionised astronomy.

       Galileo Galilei, born in 1564, made many discoveries. He used one of the first ever telescopes to observe the moon, Venus, the sun and Jupiter. He discovered four moons around Jupiter, now known as the Galilean satellites . His downfall came when he published works that agreed with the heliocentric theory. This was in direct conflict with the views of the church. The church summoned him and on the pain of death, he was forced to give up his work in astronomy and recant his views.

       In 1642, when Galileo died, another astronomer - Sir Isaac Newton was born. Newton worked in many fields but his chief contribution to astronomy were his laws of gravitation. These laws held true for four hundred years until Einstein's theory of relativity came into being.
