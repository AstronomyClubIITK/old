1st November, 2007

Observation session to be held on 1st Nov. at 10pm. We will be observing Comet Holmes, a comet which suddenly brightened up by a million times and is now visible to the naked eye. Through the telescope it appears as a small, fuzzy ball.

8th September, 2007

Lecture to be held today at 8:30 pm. Venue: L11. Topic - Hubble Space Telescope. The Hubble Space Telescope (HST) is a telescope in orbit around the Earth, named after astronomer Edwin Hubble. Its position outside the Earth's atmosphere provides significant advantages over ground-based telescopes. Since its launch in 1990, it has become one of the most important instruments in the history of astronomy. With it, astronomers have made many observations leading to breakthroughs in astrophysics. Hubble's Ultra Deep Field is the most sensitive astronomical optical image ever taken.

We will be discussing some of the most important discoveries of Hubble and showcase the Hubble slideset containing the best of Hubble pictures.

An observation session will also be held today starting from 10:00 pm. It will be a moonless night, so we will concentrate on stars, constellations, and planets like Jupiter and Mars.

13 March, 2005

The third observation session of the semester is tonight, starting at 9:30 PM. The venue is the usual one, in the cricket ground. It's a great time of year for star-gazing (and planet-gazing), and we intend to take full advantage. Saturn and Jupiter will be the highlights of course, but we also hope to observe lots of Messiers, and we'll try and get another glimpse of Comet Machholz, which has now faded to 6th magnitude. There's plenty that the heavens have to offer; come see it!

 

13 January, 2005

The second observation session of the semester is tomorrow (the 14th), starting at 10:00 PM. The venue is the usual one, in the cricket ground. We have two special things to look at. One is Comet Machholz, the brightest since Hale-Bopp, which is about 4th magnitude and a fine binocular object. The other is Saturn, currently at opposition, brighter than it will be at any time for the next 26 years. Apart from these rare sights, all our old favourites like the Orion nebula and the Beehive cluster will also be visible. It looks all set to be a memorable session. Don't miss it! 

8 September, 2004

The first observation session of the semester is on Friday (the 10th), starting at 9:30 PM. The venue is the usual one, in the cricket ground. We'll be looking at a variety of objects, particularly focusing on the prominent Messiers in the rich Sagittarius-Scorpio region (where the Galactic Center lies). This area has some fine star clusters (open and globular) and nebulae. Don't miss it!
