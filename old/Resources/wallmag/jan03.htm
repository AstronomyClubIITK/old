           Researchers’ Corner

 Ballerina Skirt

The high temperature of the solar upper atmosphere generates an outward flow of the ionized coronal gas or plasma away from the Sun at typical speeds ranging from 400 to 800 kilometers per second. This outflow is known as the "solar wind." At the Earth (1 astronomical unit (AU) or 150 million kilometers away from the Sun), 1 cubic centimeter of solar wind contains about 8 protons and an equal number of electrons (so there is no net electrical charge in the gas). Helium and heavier ions are also present in the solar wind but in smaller numbers.

Since the Skylab mission in 1973, it has been realized that the solar wind does not flow uniformly from everywhere on the Sun.

 Measurements have shown that it comes mainly from low-density regions in the corona called

"Coronal holes." Coronal holes are so-named because they appear dark in the x-ray images of the Sun (see the example below). They are usually located in the Sun's polar regions, but their irregular boundaries can dip into low solar latitudes where they affect conditions in interplanetary space.                                    

Coronal magnetic fields are constantly being carried with the solar wind into interplanetary space. The solar rotation winds up the field into a spiral resembling the water streams from a rotating garden sprinkler because the source of the field keeps moving with the Sun.

Illustration of the "winding up" of the magnetic field in the solar wind (courtesy of J. Luhmann, University of California at Berkeley).

 

At the Earth's distance from the Sun, the typical interplanetary magnetic field strength is about 5 nano teslas, or about 1/10,000 the strength of the Earth's magnetic field at the surface. The "polarity" of the interplanetary magnetic field depends on the direction of the coronal field at its roots. As illustrated below, the interplanetary field is typically organized into hemispheres of inward and outward field corresponding to the North and South magnetic poles of the solar field. The two hemispheres are separated by a sheet-like boundary carrying an electrical current. The interplanetary magnetic field directions reverse with the Sun's magnetic polarity near sunspot maxima.

Sketch showing the solar connections of the interplanetary current sheet. The directions of the magnetic fields (arrows) define their "polarity" (from: The Solar Data Analysis Center, NASA Goddard Space Flight Center).

 

Since the solar field is not a perfect configuration with simple North and South poles aligned along the Sun's rotation axis, the current sheet often has ripples like a “BALLERINA’S SKIRT”.



 

The interplanetary current sheet shape appears like a twirling ballerina's skirt when the solar dipole magnetic field has an axis different from the solar rotation axis (from: Lund Space Weather and AI Center, Lund University, Sweden).

 

The passage of this current sheet is an important marker for space weather. During solar maximum, the structure of the current sheet can become quite complicated as the magnetic fields of active regions on the Sun disrupt this simple picture

 Answers 

Answers to the quiz in the third issue of Cygnus 

1.      Moon receives some light due to reflection from the earth. This outlines the whole disc sometimes on a half moon day.

2.      The particles of solar wind are trapped by earth’s magnetic field, whose lines emerge from the North Pole, extend through the space and fall in the South Pole. When these trapped particles strike the upper atmosphere, lighting is seen and this is known as aurora.

3.      The sunlight reaches us till approximately 20 minutes after the sunset because of refraction and scattering through the atmosphere.

4.      Mercury has no atmosphere to hold the heat, while the atmosphere of Venus has a large amount of Carbon Dioxide in it. This traps the heat, as in the case of Green House Effect.

5.      A fully eclipsed moon receives light from the earth due to scattering. As the red light scatters the least, the color of the fully eclipsed moon appears red.

 COSMOS

COSMOS is the event by Astronomy Club in Techkriti and has been an integral part of it right from its inception in 1999.

The workshops, lectures and competitions in it have been attracting participation from common junta as well as from amateur astronomers. The details of COSMOS in earlier years can be found in the “About The Club” section on our web-site www.iitk.ac.in/ac.

COSMOS is there for you even in this year’s Techkriti with all its colours. You do not have to be a hardcore astronomer to find your place into it and if you are one, you will find equally good opportunities to showcase your knowledge and capacities.

The various events, which are confirmed by now, include Telescope Making Workshop, Astronomy Quiz, Astro-photography competition and Astronomy Discussion Series.

In case you have attended our workshop earlier last semester involving Telescope Assembling, do not confuse it with that. This time the event is going to involve making telescope right from the scratch, thereby making new telescopes for one-fourth the normal cost, simply by spending few weekends on it.  The first phase of the workshop will be open only for IITK people and will start a week or two before Techkriti. Here participants will do grinding of lenses and other elementary work. In the next phase, the assembling (and possibly aluminizing also) will be done. This phase will be open to all the participants. 6” reflector telescopes will be made (And that’s a great deal!!). Professionals will conduct the workshop.

Quiz will be there providing everyone an opportunity to grab their share of prizes, since there will be a lot for audience too.

But then, it is not only about what you know. Astronomy Discussion is going to be the place to tell you what you don’t know, but always wanted to know, about hundreds of things/theories/data you keep hearing and wondering about. A not to be missed part!

Rules for astrophotography competition have already been put up on the web site of Techkriti as well as Astronomy Club. In the earlier years we have got some really nice photographs for the competition. Those are available in the “Astro Gallery “ section on our site. We are expecting similar response this year too. Do not forget to submit your entries with the coordinators.

If the funds and the weather permit we are going to have balloon planetarium and Messier Hunt too. Keep checking COSMOS site at

www.iitk.ac.in/techkriti/cosmos.htm

 Enjoy, learn, win prizes and take back memorable moments with you. COSMOS is there!!
