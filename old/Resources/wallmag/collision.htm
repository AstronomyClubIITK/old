FIRST CONFIRMED STAR COLLISIONS

 

 

 

Stars are too far apart to collide with each other. That's what every basic astronomy book says, and in the Sun's part of the galaxy it's true -- fortunately for us. But in regions much richer in stars, such as dense clusters, stellar smashups occasionally ought to happen. On Wednesday, June 7th, Rex A. Saffer (Villanova University) announced

what he called "the first direct evidence of stellar collisions and

mergers."

 

Saffer studied so-called "blue straggler" stars in the nearby globular cluster NGC 6397. Blue stragglers are main-sequence stars that have too much mass -- and therefore shine too bright and blue -- for the age of the cluster in which they reside. For instance, NGC 6397 is so old that all of its stars with more than 0.8 times the mass of the Sun should have already evolved off the main sequence, become giants, and died. But the blue stragglers haven't done so. This means that somehow, they must have gained their large masses relatively recently.

 

The theory all along has been that this happens when two low-mass stars collide and merge into one. Such mergers could happen two ways, Saffer explained. Members of a binary star could be perturbed and thrown together if a third star gets captured into the system. Or two free-flying stars could bang into each other directly.

 

Direct collisions probably created five blue stragglers in the cluster's dense core, said Saffer. Four of these all have about 1.6 solar masses, twice the maximum allowed for any star that has been around since the cluster's birth. The fifth contains about 2.4 solar masses, meaning it must have formed by the coalescence of three or more stars.

 

The merging of binaries should produce a wider variety of masses than these values. A binary origin probably does account for the blue

stragglers found in the cluster's outer regions, said Saffer, but at the core, "the mass distribution argues strongly for a direct collisional origin."
