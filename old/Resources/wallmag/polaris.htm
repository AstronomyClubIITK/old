POLARIS'S VARIABLE NATURE

 

 

One of the first things Northern Hemisphere stargazers learn is to find Polaris, the North Star. Everybody knows that when you find it, you're looking north. What surprises some stargazers is that Polaris is slightly variable, as astronomers have known for many decades. In fact, it is a Cepheid variable -- a class of pulsating star whose light output (luminosity) depends on the pulsation period. Polaris's period is 4 days, and on this basis it was predicted to be about 38 times the size of the Sun.

 

Polaris appears to be what is called an "overtone pulsator." Regular Cepheids get brighter and dimmer because their outer layers expand and contract in a uniform manner. But in 1997, a group of African and European astronomers discovered that when Polaris pulsates, not all of its bulk expands and contracts at the same time.

 

In a paper given at the AAS meeting Wednesday, a team of astronomers using the Navy Prototype Optical Interferometer in Flagstaff, Arizona, presented a measurement showing that Polaris is actually 45 times larger than the Sun. The difference stems from the fact that Polaris, like a few other Cepheids, is pulsating in a complex mode.
